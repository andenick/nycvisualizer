Transit Access & Job Reachability — Best Answers

What this is. A network-based (real street + transit routing, via OpenTripPlanner) measure of how many NYC jobs a resident can reach within 45 minutes of a weekday 08:00 trip using walking + subway/bus/ferry, computed for a 1,196-cell H3 res-8 grid covering NYC land and mapped down to all 37,588 census blocks, then summarized by block-group median-income decile.

Because reachability here is measured over the actual transit + street network (not straight-line distance), the usual "as-the-crow-flies overstates access" caveat does not apply. The honesty issue that does matter is departure time (this is an AM-peak snapshot) — see caveats.

Baseline: 4,581,241 NYC census-block jobs (LODES WAC 2023). Population-weighted using 2020 Census block population. Coverage: 37,507 / 37,588 blocks carry a value; 81 blocks (0.2%) fall in 17 grid cells whose centroid landed in water/parkland with no routable street and are left null (never fabricated).

Claim 1 — The typical New Yorker reaches ~19% of the city's jobs in 45 minutes; the median differs ~40× across boroughs.

Population-weighted mean jobs reachable ≤45 min (wk-08:00):

Borough Mean jobs ≤45 min ≈ % of NYC jobs
Manhattan 2,219,501 48%
Brooklyn 690,705 15%
Queens 574,593 13%
Bronx 349,418 8%
Staten Island 56,094 1.2%
Citywide 861,039 18.8%

Caveat: Manhattan's figure is inflated by proximity to the job core itself (short trips reach the densest job cluster). Staten Island — no subway, served only by the SIR, buses, and the ferry — is the clear structural outlier. The dominant access divide in NYC is geographic (borough/proximity-to-Manhattan) at least as much as income.

Claim 2 — Job access rises with income, but the gradient is concentrated at the top, not a smooth ladder (AM-peak snapshot).

Population-weighted mean jobs reachable ≤45 min by income decile (D1 = lowest income):

Decile Median-income band Mean jobs ≤45 min % of NYC jobs
D1 $2.5k–$38k 742,615 16.2%
D2 $38k–$53k 591,238 12.9%
D3–D7 $53k–$108k ~600k–650k 13–14%
D8 $108k–$128k 799,713 17.5%
D9 $128k–$166k 1,238,422 27.0%
D10 $166k–$250k 1,998,087 43.6%

D10/D1 ratio = 2.69×. But deciles 1 through 7 are essentially flat (~13–16% of jobs); the advantage is almost entirely in the top three deciles, and especially D10.

Caveat: This is not a "poverty = transit desert" story. NYC's lowest-income decile actually out-reaches deciles 2–7 — because the poorest neighborhoods (South Bronx, Harlem, central Brooklyn) sit on dense subway lines close to Manhattan. Low access concentrates in middle-income outer-borough areas (eastern Queens, south Brooklyn, Staten Island), not the poorest ones.

Claim 3 — Frequent-transit access is nearly universal (99.9%), so the equity gap is about where transit goes, not whether a stop exists nearby.

99.9% of the population lives within ~400 m of a stop with ≥8 scheduled AM-peak departures (≈ a vehicle every ≤15 min). The share is 100% for the bottom five income deciles and only dips to ~99.6–99.8% at the top (a few low-density wealthy edges).

Caveat: "A frequent stop is nearby" ≠ "many destinations are reachable." The reachability gap in Claims 1–2 exists despite near-universal stop access — it is driven by network structure (radial lines converging on Manhattan; weak outer-borough-to-outer-borough links), not by stop scarcity. The flag counts combined stop frequency (any route/direction), not per-route headway.

Claim 4 — Reachability spans 0 to ~3.06 million jobs across the grid; the median cell reaches ~138k.

Across the 1,179 successfully-routed origin cells, jobs reachable ≤45 min ranges from ~0 (isolated edges) to 3,061,991 (Midtown/Lower-Manhattan cells), median 137,643. The distribution is highly right-skewed — a small set of Manhattan-core and inner-Brooklyn cells accounts for the top of the range.

Caveat: Origin granularity is res-8 (~0.7 km²); a block inherits its cell's isochrone, so sub-cell variation is not resolved.

Cross-cutting caveats (read before citing)

  1. AM-peak only. The precomputed grid is a weekday 08:00 snapshot. Reachability is materially departure-time sensitive — midday and especially evening/late-night service is sparser, so off-peak access (and the equity gap) will differ. The live /api/isochrone endpoint supports noon and evening windows for on-demand queries.
  2. NYC jobs only. The routing graph is clipped to the NYC bbox and only NYC census blocks are counted, so jobs in NJ / Westchester / Long Island reachable within 45 min are excluded — this understates access for near-border origins (e.g. jobs in Jersey City / Newark from western Manhattan and Staten Island).
  3. City modes only. LIRR and Metro-North are not in the v1 graph; commuter-rail reachability (which would lift access for eastern Queens / the Bronx) is not modeled.
  4. No fares. Pure walk+transit travel time; fare cost and fare-based transfer rules are not applied.
  5. Walk speed fixed at 1.33 m/s (OTP default); no mobility/accessibility variation.
