FINDINGS — NYC Sidewalk Coverage, Equity, Condition & Accessibility (B5.1)

Best-Answers brief — each claim carries its caveat and pointer. Method detail: Technical/NYCPlatform/analysis/sidewalk/METHODS.md. All measurement EPSG:2263 ftUS; sidewalk geometry is the DCP planimetric layer (2022 aerial flight).

1. 85% of NYC's pedestrian street segments have sidewalks on both sides; only 3% have none. Of 96,553 CSCL pedestrian roadway segments: 82,074 both sides, 11,630 one side, 2,849 none. Caveat: side test uses the segment chord — curved segments can be side-misassigned; planimetric = 2022 vintage. Pointer: 01_coverage_segments.parquet, 01_coverage_borough_summary.xlsx, fig01_coverage_by_borough.png.

2. Sidewalk absence is a Staten Island and Queens phenomenon: 62% of no-sidewalk segments are in those two boroughs. none counts: Staten Island 907 (6.2% of its segments), Queens 867, Bronx 553, Brooklyn 355, Manhattan 167 (1.9%). Caveat: some none segments are legitimately sidewalk-free (parkland edges, private/paper streets). Pointer: 01_coverage_segments.parquet filtered coverage_class='none'.

3. Manhattan sidewalks are the city's widest (median ~12.9 ft per segment); Staten Island's the narrowest (~8.4 ft); citywide median ~10.1 ft. Caveat: widths are a 2Area/Perimeter proxy for the medial-axis method (Meli Harvey 2020, sidewalkwidths.nyc) — validation vs max-inscribed width: r = 0.47, proxy ~0.69x inscribed; treat as a relative signal, weakest on plazas and corner-wrapping polygons. Pointer:* 02_width_segments.parquet, fig02_width_by_borough.png, fig02_width_validation.png.

4. The equity gradient is a crowding story, not a coverage story: the poorest fifth of blocks has the HIGHEST sidewalk coverage per frontage foot (12.3 vs ~9.4-10.4) but the LEAST sidewalk per person (47 sqft/capita vs 76 in Q4). Low-income areas are denser and better-covered per foot of street, but each resident shares far less sidewalk. Caveat: block-group income imputed to blocks; per-capita uses nighttime (residential) population — Manhattan CBD daytime crowding is understated. Pointer: 03_block_equity.xlsx (income_quintiles sheet), fig03_percapita_by_quintile.png; block-level r(income, coverage) = -0.11.

5. 81 populated census blocks (>=50 residents; 6,980 people total) have ZERO mapped sidewalk area — led by a 310-person block in Concourse (Bronx), and clusters in Breezy Point/Broad Channel (Queens), Todt Hill/Annadale (Staten Island), and Brighton Beach (Brooklyn). Caveat: some are gated/private-street communities (Breezy Point) or tower-in-park blocks where paths aren't planimetric "sidewalk"; zero mapped != zero walkable. Pointer: 03_block_equity.xlsx (zero_sidewalk_gaps sheet).

6. The remote condition signal is real: 311 sidewalk-complaint density and DOT field-violation density correlate at r = 0.87 across the city's 71 community-district areas. The composite condition index (311 + violations + tree damage) ranks Park Slope/Carroll Gardens (BK06), Bed-Stuy (BK03), Upper East Side (MN08), Bensonhurst (BK11), Bushwick (BK04) as worst. Caveat: 311 reflects reporting propensity as well as condition; violations are a 1990s-> stock with data-entry date outliers; tree damage flags are 2015-vintage. Pointer: 04_condition_cdta.xlsx, fig04_311_vs_dot_validation.png, fig04_worst_condition_cds.png.

7. Street trees are a first-order sidewalk-condition driver: in the worst community districts ~37-44% of rated street trees carry sidewalk-damage flags, and 36.3% of violation properties later show a DOT repair record. Caveat: the violation->repair link is via DOT's internal property id (bblid), which is NOT a Buildings BBL — property-level joins outside DOT's own tables are impossible. Pointer: 04_condition_cdta.parquet (tree_damage_pct), validation sheet in 04_condition_nta.xlsx.

8. 87.5% of NYC's 48,717 street intersections have at least one pedestrian ramp within 50 ft — but 6,086 do not, concentrated in Staten Island (77.1% coverage) and the Bronx (83.8%) vs ~91% in Manhattan/Brooklyn. Caveat: "has a ramp" != "all corners ramped"; 50-ft radius slightly under-counts at very wide intersections. Pointer: 05_accessibility_intersections.parquet, fig05_ada_gap_map.png.

9. Of ramps that exist and are measured, 25.9% fail the ADA 8.33% running-slope maximum (54,966 of 212,194); Staten Island is worst (33.1% of its ramps steep). So the accessibility gap is roughly one part missing ramps, three parts substandard ramps. Caveat: slope values >=100 are sentinels and excluded; survey vintage varies by DOT collection cycle. Pointer: 05_accessibility_intersections.xlsx (ada_slope + borough sheets).

10. Lowest-coverage populated NTAs are the auto-oriented periphery: New Springville-Willowbrook (SI), Todt Hill (SI), Riverdale-Spuyten Duyvil (BX), Tottenville-Charleston (SI), Annadale-Woodrow (SI) — all under 5.3 sqft of sidewalk per frontage foot vs ~10 citywide. Caveat: frontage proxy = block perimeter; park/shoreline edges bias these ratios down somewhat. Pointer: 03_nta_coverage.parquet, fig03_coverage_vs_income.png.

Data-quality discoveries logged for the platform: (a) sidewalk_violations.bblid/sidewalk_built.bblid is an internal DOT id, not a BBL — 0% PLUTO join; borough is recoverable from onfrtocode street codes (96.9% CD-geolocatable). (b) qol_sr311_sidewalk is 81% Noise - Street/Sidewalk — any condition use must exclude it. (c) geo_ramps.RAMP_RUNNING_SLOPE_TOTAL uses 999-style sentinels. (d) CSCL Street Width null on 8,610 segments (default 30 ft applied). (e) 2015 tree census nta codes are 2010-vintage NTAs — spatial join used instead.
